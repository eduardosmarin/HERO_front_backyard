Description:
Abstract:
These data represent tree cover for two cities (Chelsea and the urban core of Holyoke) in Massachusetts. Tree cover was manually delineated on aerial imagery from 1952, 1971, 2003 and 2014. Also included are neighborhood boundaries for both of these study locations.
Purpose:
Urban foresters assess urban tree canopy cover trends using remotely sensed imagery as a way to provide data for municipalities to set or maintain goals for tree canopy. However, these assessments typically do not take into account historical and socioeconomical dynamics of tree cover change over many decades. These land cover change data were collected to assess the rates and drivers of tree canopy cover change, using Holyoke and Chelsea to investigate the processes of tree cover change in post-industrial cities. Understanding historical drivers of tree canopy cover change can be used to inform multi-decadal urban tree canopy assessments and the creation of targeted, feasible urban tree canopy goals at neighborhood and city scales. These historical analyses can help urban natural resource managers to better understand how to protect and expand their cities’ urban tree canopy over time.

Description_of_Geographic_Extent:
Chelsea, MA is located directly north of Boston, MA across the Mystic River and is part of Suffolk County. Chelsea is the smallest city in Massachusetts and also one of the most densely populated. Holyoke, MA is located in western Massachusetts along the banks of the Connecticut River and is part of Hampden County. Holyoke was one of the earliest planned industrial cities in the United States due to the construction of a dam and a series of canals which led to many different manufacturing companies. Only the urban core of Holyoke was included so that the study areas from both cities could be more consistent in size, land cover and fit to historical neighborhood boundaries.

Logical_Consistency_Report:
One individual delineated all polygons for the cities, which promotes internal consistency. A second interpreter also re-delineated 10% of the study area. The percent agreement between interpreters and both cities was over 93% agreement. The average difference in interpreters’ tree cover estimates across all years in Chelsea was 0.54 percentage points and 1.80 percentage points in Holyoke.
Completeness_Report:
There are no missing data in this package. For the purposes of this study, only the urban core of Holyoke was included so that the study areas from both cities could be more consistent in size, land cover and fit to historical neighborhood boundaries.

Process_Step:
Process_Description:
Aerial photos from years 1952 and 1971 were digitally cropped to remove registration marks and edge distortion. Next, they were georeferenced using USGS imagery from 2014 with 30 centimeter (cm) resolution. This USGS imagery was selected because of its higher resolution than the NAIP imagery (1 meter [m]) used in this project, and because it shares the same projection as the NAIP imagery: NAD83/UTM Zone 18N (for western Massachusetts) or 19N (for eastern Massachusetts). Second or third order polynomial transformations were applied to the images based on levels of alignment and distortion which led to an average RMSE of 2.97 m.

We defined a set of six rules for manual delineation of tree cover. These rules helped ensure consistency between interpreters and across cities and years. For each city, tree cover was delineated first on the oldest imagery, then moving forward in time, allowing older imagery to assist in the interpretation of newer imagery. We included both tree and shrub cover in tree cover delineation due to the impracticality of distinguishing trees and shrubs in two-dimensional images.

1. Tree canopy polygon area must be greater than or equal to 7 square meter (m²). Polygons smaller than this can be removed post delineation.

2. Remove treeless gaps within a canopy cover polygon if groundcover is identifiable and the gaps are not dominated by shadow, e.g., open space in a forested patch.

3. Use shadow to aid in discernment of tree canopy versus shrub (i.e., a juvenile tree would produce a visible, angled shadow denoting its height, while low-to-ground shrubs would not have this trait).

4. Use visual obstruction of objects (i.e., sidewalks, rooflines, roads) by tree cover to aid in identification.

5. Edges of delineated tree canopy that extend beyond the study area boundary should be clipped to said boundary post delineation.

6. A variable viewing scale of 1:400 to 1:1000 was used across all imagery during delineation.

Overview_Description:
Entity_and_Attribute_Overview:
Below you will find a list and description of the files included in this data publication. Data are provided as individual Esri ArcGIS shapefiles (SHP) (associated files also included), and then all of the data for each location (Chelsea and Holyoke) are combined and provided as a single OGC Geopackage (GPKG).

Tree canopy cover data include:
FID = unique polygon indicator
Shape = indicator that the shape is a polygon
Area_SqM = area of the tree cover polygon in square meters

Neighborhood data include:
FID = unique polygon indicator
Shape = indicator that the shape is a polygon
Neighbrhd or Name = name of each neighborhood
AreaSqM = area of the neighborhood polygon in square meters
